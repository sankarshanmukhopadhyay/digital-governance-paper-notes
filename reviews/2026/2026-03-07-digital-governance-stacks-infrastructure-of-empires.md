## Review

This essay examines the geopolitical implications of exporting digital governance stacks and raises an important question: can externally designed governance infrastructures create new forms of administrative dependence for states adopting them? The paper argues that as governments increasingly rely on integrated digital systems to administer identity, welfare, taxation, and regulation, the infrastructure underlying those systems becomes politically consequential. When these governance layers are designed, financed, or standardized externally, the resulting architectures may embed asymmetric influence or structural dependence.

The paper’s main strength lies in reframing DPI and e‑government platforms as political infrastructure rather than neutral technical systems. By situating digital governance within a broader geopolitical context, the essay highlights how infrastructure choices may shape sovereignty, administrative autonomy, and policy control. The argument is timely, particularly as governments worldwide accelerate adoption of DPI and AI‑enabled administrative tools.

Methodologically, however, the work functions more as a conceptual policy essay than a formal research study. Its argument relies primarily on interpretive synthesis, geopolitical analogy, and illustrative examples rather than a clearly defined research design or systematic case analysis. Key concepts such as “governance stack,” “onshoring governance,” and “imperialistic governance practices” are not operationalized, making it difficult to evaluate the strength of causal claims.

Another limitation is the lack of architectural specificity. Governance risks typically arise from concrete system design choices such as hosting control, rule‑update authority, auditability, procurement terms, and reversibility. Without examining these components, the analysis risks treating very different technological arrangements as equivalent forms of dependence.

Despite these limitations, the essay makes a valuable contribution by pushing DPI debates beyond efficiency and inclusion toward questions of sovereignty and institutional resilience. With clearer definitions, comparative case studies, and a structured framework for assessing infrastructure governance risks, the argument could evolve from a provocative warning into a rigorous analytical model for evaluating digital governance architectures.

## Key Insight

Digital governance infrastructure is not merely technical plumbing; its architecture can shape sovereignty, administrative autonomy, and geopolitical influence.
