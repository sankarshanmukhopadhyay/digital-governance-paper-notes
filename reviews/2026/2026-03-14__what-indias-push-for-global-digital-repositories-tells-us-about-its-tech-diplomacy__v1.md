# Paper Review

## Review

This article offers a sharp and timely interpretation of India’s recent push for global digital repositories, including the Global DPI Repository, the proposed AI commons, and a cybersecurity cooperation portal. Its core argument is that these initiatives are not merely technical or developmental artifacts. They are diplomatic instruments. By advancing repositories rather than hard normative frameworks, India can project leadership across the Global South, demonstrate institutional initiative, and avoid locking itself into a rigid ideological camp in contests over AI governance, cyber norms, and digital sovereignty.

The piece is strongest when it links this “repository diplomacy” to India’s longer-standing pursuit of strategic autonomy. That framing is persuasive. It explains why India would prefer low-friction, voluntary, capacity-building platforms over binding commitments or doctrinally thick positions on contentious questions such as cyber sovereignty, multistakeholder governance, or an AI “third way.” In that sense, the article is valuable for public-interest technology and DPI debates because it treats infrastructure diplomacy as political strategy rather than neutral development theater.

Its main limitation is methodological. This is a conceptual policy essay, not an empirical study. The claims are plausible, but they rest on interpretive synthesis rather than comparative evidence showing how these repositories have actually shaped partnerships, standards uptake, financing, or implementation behavior. The article also leaves underexplored the governance design of the repositories themselves: who curates entries, what quality thresholds apply, how harms are documented, and whether affected communities can contest the framing of a “best practice.” That is where the real governance action lives, and where repository models can easily drift into narrative laundering.

Even so, the article makes an important contribution. It warns that repositories can widen access to policy knowledge while simultaneously flattening political conflict, obscuring institutional trade-offs, and exporting legitimacy without exporting accountability. For DPI, DPG, and AI governance practitioners, that is the real takeaway: repositories are not substitutes for transfer of capacity, financing, safeguards, critique, or public accountability. They are governance instruments, and should be evaluated as such.

## Key Insight

India’s repository diplomacy is best understood not as neutral knowledge-sharing, but as a low-commitment instrument for projecting leadership while preserving strategic flexibility in a fragmented technology order.
