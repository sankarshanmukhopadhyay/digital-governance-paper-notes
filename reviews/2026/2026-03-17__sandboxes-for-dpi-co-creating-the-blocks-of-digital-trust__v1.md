# Paper Review

## Review

This report makes a strong and overdue argument: DPI should not be built through swagger, vendor decks, and national-scale improvisation. It should be tested upstream through structured experimentation. By mapping 14 national and 2 regional sandbox efforts across identity, payments, and data exchange, the report gives the field something it badly needs: an empirical baseline for how governments are actually using controlled experimentation to reduce risk and learn before rollout.

Its core contribution is conceptual clarity. The report defines DPI sandboxes as controlled learning environments and usefully distinguishes regulatory, operational, and hybrid models. That matters because too much DPI discussion still treats technology design and governance design as separate planets. The report shows they are entangled. Identity, payments, and data exchange are not neutral pipes. They are institutional choices that shape inclusion, trust, sovereignty, interoperability, and contestability.

The strongest parts are the sections on trust, rights, and irreversibility. The report correctly notes that bad DPI does not merely fail. It calcifies. Once identity systems, payment rails, or data-sharing layers are embedded into state practice, governance mistakes become expensive and politically sticky. Sandboxes therefore matter not because they are trendy, but because they create a mechanism for institutional learning before exclusion, surveillance, or dependency becomes infrastructural.

The limitation is that the report remains more descriptive than evaluative. It maps sandbox rationales and typologies well, but is lighter on the hard question of effectiveness. Which sandboxes actually changed procurement, architecture, legal design, or deployment decisions? Which became symbolic consultation exercises? Which produced measurable improvements in inclusion, resilience, or public trust? That evidence layer is thinner than it should be.

Even so, the report is strategically valuable. It reframes sandboxing as a state capability for trustworthy DPI rather than a sidecar for innovation policy. That is the right frame. The next increment should be sharper metrics, stronger failure analysis, and more explicit pathways for translating sandbox outputs into binding institutional decisions.

## Key Insight

The report’s most useful move is to treat sandboxes not as innovation theater, but as upstream governance capacity for testing trust, inclusion, interoperability, and institutional learning before DPI choices harden at population scale.
