# Paper Review

## Review

This report is a useful comparative map of national digital identity systems, especially for policymakers trying to move beyond vendor pitch decks and abstract principles. Its core contribution is not a grand theory of identity, but a grounded synthesis of what repeatedly makes national eID systems work across 42 countries: clear governance, interoperable technical foundations, strong security and trust, and integration into everyday services. That framing is pragmatic and broadly correct. It treats digital identity as infrastructure for administration, welfare, finance, and service delivery rather than as a standalone credentialing exercise.

The report is strongest when it surfaces implementation patterns across jurisdictions and links adoption to institutional design. It correctly emphasizes that uptake is shaped by service relevance, usability, legal clarity, and trust, not simply by technical rollout. For public-interest readers, this matters because it shifts the discussion from "build a wallet" to "build an accountable ecosystem around identity use."

Its limits are equally important. The methodology is expert-informed and comparative, but not deeply causal. It relies on mixed country evidence, indicative service counts, and partially non-comparable adoption measures. That makes the report strong for directional insight and benchmarking, but weaker as a basis for hard ranking or policy transfer. It also underplays political economy questions such as exclusion by design, function creep, vendor dependence, and the governance risks that arise when high-coverage identity becomes de facto mandatory.

Even so, the report lands an important operational truth: digital identity should be governed as a reusable trust layer within public digital infrastructure. The best systems are not merely secure or widely issued. They are embedded in high-frequency services, adaptable to regulatory change, legible to users, and constrained by trust-preserving rules. For governments pursuing DPI, that is the real lesson. Identity is not the product. Institutional legitimacy at the point of use is.

## Key Insight

Digital identity succeeds not when a credential exists, but when governance, interoperability, trust, and everyday service relevance are engineered together as public infrastructure.
