# Paper Review

## Review

The Doot white paper proposes a national architecture for citizen-owned AI agents that act as personal digital representatives capable of interacting with government and private services on behalf of individuals. The proposed system, called Agent One, builds on India’s Digital Public Infrastructure rails such as Aadhaar identity, DigiLocker documents, UPI payments, and ABHA health records. Each citizen would have a personal agent rooted in their digital identity and authorized through consent-based delegation.

The paper’s core insight is that the next stage of digital public infrastructure may not be additional portals or applications but an agent layer that mediates citizen interaction with complex administrative systems. The architecture embeds governance primitives directly into the technical design, including identity-bound delegation, least-privilege consent, policy-bounded tool execution, audit logs, and human-in-the-loop authorization for high-risk actions.

The design emphasizes an edge-first computing model in which a citizen’s primary agent runs on personal devices while a cloud replica supports complex tasks and coordination. This aims to balance privacy, resilience, and computational capability.

While conceptually compelling, the proposal remains largely architectural and aspirational. It lacks empirical validation, quantitative evaluation, and a detailed institutional governance framework. Key questions remain around liability, agent certification, economic sustainability, and civil liberties risks associated with identity-anchored agents.

Despite these limitations, the paper advances an important idea for public-interest technology: AI agents may become a new infrastructure layer mediating access to services, markets, and state institutions. The long-term governance challenge will be ensuring these agents operate as citizen representatives rather than platform intermediaries.

## Key Insight

Citizen-owned AI agents could become the next interface layer of digital public infrastructure, reshaping how individuals interact with governments and markets.
