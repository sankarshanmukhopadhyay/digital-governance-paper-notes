## Review

This is a compelling strategic frame: an open, resilient, “non-aligned” AI posture as an alternative to the US–China stack race. As geopolitics, it is thoughtful. As execution doctrine, it is still incomplete. And that gap is where most “sovereignty” stories go to die.

We have already accepted the obvious: sovereign AI is not self-sufficiency. It is agency under interdependence. But the uncomfortable truth is that many countries are mostly doing dependency with better branding.

“Open” is not a plan. “Resilient” is not a mechanism. And “non-aligned” does not magically remove leverage.

Without concrete design choices, an open stack becomes a shared dependency stack. Without enforceable rights, cooperation becomes coordinated reliance. Without tested exits, resilience is just a slogan.

The missing layer is the part that actually hurts: optionality engineering.

Can you switch providers in weeks, not years?
Do you have audit and benchmark rights, not just “trust us” dashboards?
Is portability real across models, data pipelines, and safety tooling?
Can you revoke access and have it propagate downstream?
Are logs forensic-grade, and is redress operational rather than compliance theatre?

Interoperability is not “open APIs.” It is procurement-grade portability: contracts, interfaces, and drills that make leaving feasible. Otherwise “non-aligned AI” risks becoming sovereignty-as-a-service, a polite wrapper over hyperscaler gravity.

The essential bit in the article is reframing sovereignty as resilience, not isolation. But resilience is not declared. It is engineered, rehearsed, and stress-tested.

Bottom line: sovereignty is not a press release. It is a practiced capability. If you cannot practice egress, verify claims, and enforce redress, you do not have autonomy. You have access. And access is not control.

## Key Insight

Sovereignty in AI is not a branding posture but an operational capability built through portability, audit rights, egress drills, and enforceable redress.
